# Heart Rate Training for Runners

## Why Train by Heart Rate?

Pace-based training ignores the body's actual physiological state. On a hot humid day, the same effort costs more cardiovascular work — your heart rate will be 5-10 bpm higher at the same pace. Training by heart rate means training by actual stress load, not just speed.

Heart rate training is especially effective for:
- Building aerobic base without overtraining
- Preventing easy runs from drifting too fast
- Monitoring recovery and readiness
- Long-term health monitoring (tracking resting HR trends)

## Calculating Your Heart Rate Zones

**Step 1: Find your max HR**
- Most accurate: perform a max HR test (5 min hard run followed by 1 min all-out sprint)
- Formula estimate: 220 - age (unreliable for individuals, use only as starting point)
- Practical estimate: highest HR seen in any recent hard effort

**Step 2: Calculate zones from max HR**

| Zone | % Max HR | Name | Feel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zone 1 | 50-60% | Recovery | Very easy, barely breathing harder |
| Zone 2 | 60-70% | Aerobic / Easy | Easy, can speak full sentences |
| Zone 3 | 70-80% | Moderate / Tempo | Harder, can speak only short phrases |
| Zone 4 | 80-90% | Threshold | Hard, only single words |
| Zone 5 | 90-100% | Anaerobic / Max | All-out, cannot speak |

**Example (max HR = 185 bpm):**
- Zone 1: 93-111 bpm
- Zone 2: 111-130 bpm
- Zone 3: 130-148 bpm
- Zone 4: 148-167 bpm
- Zone 5: 167-185 bpm

## The Importance of Zone 2 (Easy Aerobic)

Zone 2 running is the most under-appreciated training tool. At 60-70% max HR:
- Mitochondrial density increases (the engine capacity of your muscle cells)
- Fat oxidation capacity improves (crucial for marathon fueling)
- Capillary density improves (better oxygen delivery)
- Recovery is fast — you can run Zone 2 every day without accumulating fatigue

**The Zone 2 test**: you should be able to hold a full conversation. If you can't, slow down.

Most recreational runners run their easy days 10-15 bpm too fast, turning recovery runs into moderate-effort runs that accumulate fatigue without providing quality stimulus.

## Heart Rate Drift (Cardiac Drift)

On runs over 60 minutes, heart rate naturally rises even at constant pace — this is cardiac drift. Causes: dehydration, heat, cumulative muscle fatigue. Normal drift is 5-10 bpm over 60-90 min. Excessive drift (15+ bpm) indicates:
- Insufficient hydration
- Running in heat beyond adaptation
- Underlying fatigue from the previous day

Strategy: run by perceived effort or pace on hot days, don't chase a heart rate number upward.

## Resting Heart Rate as Recovery Indicator

Your resting HR (measured first thing in the morning before getting up) is a reliable recovery signal:
- Baseline: establish over 2-3 weeks of normal training
- **+5-7 bpm above baseline**: mild fatigue, reduce intensity that day
- **+8-10 bpm above baseline**: significant fatigue, replace hard session with easy run or rest
- **+10+ bpm above baseline**: potential illness or heavy overtraining — full rest

## Heart Rate and Overtraining

Signs of cardiovascular overtraining:
- Resting HR trending up over multiple weeks despite consistent sleep
- HR slow to recover after hard efforts (takes 3-5 min to drop 30 bpm when it used to take 90 seconds)
- Same pace effort produces higher HR than it did 4 weeks ago
- HR "decoupling" during long runs (pace drops but HR keeps rising)

If you see these patterns in your data, take 1-2 easy weeks before resuming hard training.

## Practical HR Training Tips

1. Don't look at HR during the first 5 min of any run — it takes time to stabilize.
2. For treadmill or track work, use pace. Reserve HR monitoring for outdoor easy runs and long runs.
3. Review your average HR trend across a training block — it should not increase week over week at the same paces.
4. A resting HR below 50 bpm is a positive sign of aerobic fitness development after months of consistent training.
