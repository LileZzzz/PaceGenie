# Nutrition for Runners

## Daily Fueling Principles

Running nutrition is not complicated but requires consistency. The three non-negotiables:

1. **Carbohydrates are the primary fuel**: Glycogen (stored carbohydrate) powers both easy and hard runs. Chronic carb restriction leads to poor training quality, slow recovery, and increased injury risk. Target: 5-7 g/kg body weight on training days, 3-5 g/kg on rest days.

2. **Protein for repair**: Running breaks down muscle. Protein rebuilds it. Target: 1.6-2.0 g/kg body weight daily. Distribute across meals — the body can only absorb ~30-40 g per meal effectively.

3. **Fat is not the enemy**: Dietary fat supports hormone production, vitamin absorption, and sustained energy during easy runs. Aim for 25-35% of daily calories from healthy fats.

## Pre-Run Nutrition

**2-3 hours before (full meal):**
- Carb-heavy, moderate protein, low fat and fiber
- Examples: rice + chicken, pasta + tomato sauce, oats + banana
- Avoid high-fat or high-fiber foods that slow digestion

**30-60 minutes before (small snack):**
- Fast-digesting carbs only: banana, white toast with honey, sports drink
- Skip protein and fat — they slow gastric emptying
- For runs under 60 minutes, no pre-run snack needed

**Race morning:** Eat what you've practiced in training. Race day is not the time to experiment.

## During-Run Fueling

| Run Duration | Fueling Strategy |
|---|---|
| Under 60 min | Water only, no calories needed |
| 60-90 min | Optional 20-30 g carbs (half a gel or sports drink) |
| 90 min - 2.5 hours | 30-60 g carbs per hour, start at 45 min |
| Marathon (2.5+ hours) | 60-90 g carbs per hour, practice gut training in training |

**Hydration during runs:**
- Drink to thirst for runs under 90 min
- For longer runs: 400-600 ml per hour in cool weather, 600-800 ml in heat
- Electrolytes essential for runs over 90 min (sodium is most important)

## Post-Run Recovery Nutrition

The 30-60 minute window after a hard run is the most important for recovery.

**The recovery formula:** 3:1 or 4:1 carbs to protein ratio
- 60-80 g carbs + 20-30 g protein within 30-60 minutes of finishing
- Examples: chocolate milk (ideal ratio naturally), rice + eggs, yogurt + fruit + granola

**Post-run hydration:** Drink 1.5× the fluid lost (estimate by weight change or urine color — target pale yellow)

## Race Week Carbohydrate Loading

Starting 3 days before a half or full marathon:
- Increase carbs to 8-10 g/kg body weight per day
- Reduce training volume (already in taper) so carbs replenish glycogen stores
- Avoid new foods, high-fiber vegetables, and excessive fat
- Expected weight gain of 1-2 kg is normal (each gram of glycogen stores 3 g of water) — do not be alarmed

## Common Nutritional Mistakes

1. **Under-fueling long runs**: Finishing a long run on empty trains the body to bonk. Fuel every 45 min.
2. **Not practicing race-day nutrition in training**: The gut needs training too. Practice your race-day gel and drink strategy during long runs.
3. **Ignoring sodium**: Sports drinks without electrolytes cause hyponatremia in long events. Use electrolyte tabs or sodium-containing gels.
4. **Cutting carbs to lose weight mid-training**: Weight loss should happen in the off-season, not during a training block. Carb restriction during training impairs adaptation.
