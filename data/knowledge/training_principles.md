# Running Training Principles

## The 80/20 Rule (Polarized Training)

Research by Stephen Seiler shows that elite endurance athletes spend roughly 80% of
their training time at low intensity (easy pace, conversational effort, heart rate Zone
1-2) and only 20% at moderate-to-high intensity. The reason this works: aerobic base
development happens most efficiently at low intensity, while high-intensity sessions
provide the stress stimulus for speed adaptation. Doing 50/50 is the most common
amateur mistake and leads to chronic fatigue without proportional fitness gain.

Practical application:
- If you run 5 days a week, 4 of those days should feel genuinely easy.
- "Easy" means you can hold a full conversation without gasping.
- Heart rate should stay below 75% of max HR for easy runs.

## Progressive Overload

The body adapts to stress only when that stress slightly exceeds current capacity.
Progressive overload means systematically increasing training load over time, but the
key word is "systematically." Random big jumps cause injury; no progression causes
stagnation.

Methods to apply progressive overload:
1. **Volume**: Add distance before adding intensity.
2. **Frequency**: Add a run day only after current days feel manageable.
3. **Intensity**: Add one quality session per week after building base.

## The 10% Rule

Never increase total weekly mileage by more than 10% week over week. This is the
single most evidence-backed rule in recreational running injury prevention. The
connective tissue (tendons, ligaments, cartilage) adapts much more slowly than
cardiovascular fitness. A runner can feel aerobically capable of more volume weeks
before their tendons can handle it — this gap is where overuse injuries happen.

Example: if you ran 40 km last week, cap this week at 44 km.

## Recovery Is Training

Adaptation does not happen during the hard session — it happens during recovery.
Sleep, easy days, and rest days are when the body rebuilds stronger. Signs that
recovery is insufficient:
- Resting heart rate elevated by 5+ bpm compared to baseline
- Persistent heavy legs that do not clear after an easy day
- Motivation to train drops noticeably
- Performance declining despite consistent training

Recommended recovery practices:
- At least one full rest day per week
- Easy run the day after any quality session
- 7-9 hours of sleep during heavy training blocks
- Deload week (reduce volume by 30-40%) every 3-4 weeks
