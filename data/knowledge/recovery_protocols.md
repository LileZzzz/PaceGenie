# Recovery Protocols for Runners

## Why Recovery Is the Missing Training Variable

Most amateur runners focus entirely on adding more training and neglect recovery. The adaptation cycle is:
**Training stress → Recovery → Adaptation (fitness improvement)**

Skip recovery and the cycle breaks: stress accumulates, adaptation stalls, injury risk rises. The best runners are not those who train the most — they are those who recover most effectively between sessions.

## Post-Run Recovery (Immediate: 0-60 Minutes)

**Nutrition window (within 30-45 minutes):**
- 60-80 g fast carbohydrates + 20-30 g protein
- Replenishes glycogen and starts muscle protein synthesis
- Easy options: chocolate milk, Greek yogurt + banana, rice cakes + turkey
- Skipping this window after runs over 60 min significantly delays recovery

**Hydration:**
- Weigh yourself before and after long runs — each 0.5 kg lost = ~500 ml fluid deficit
- Drink 1.5× the weight lost over the next 2-4 hours
- Include sodium (electrolytes) if run was over 90 min or in heat

**Mobility (5-10 min):**
- Gentle dynamic stretching or walking — not aggressive static stretching on fatigued muscles
- Hip flexors, calf/Achilles, hip rotators are priority for runners
- Static stretching can wait until fully cooled down (30+ min post-run)

## Sleep: The Most Powerful Recovery Tool

Sleep is when the body produces the majority of growth hormone and carries out tissue repair. For runners in training:

- **7-9 hours** minimum; athletes in peak training blocks benefit from 9 hours
- **Sleep quality matters as much as quantity**: consistent sleep/wake times, cool dark room
- **Napping**: 20-30 min naps between training and afternoon training are effective for two-a-day runners

Signs your training is compromising sleep quality:
- Difficulty falling asleep despite fatigue (sympathetic nervous system overactivation)
- Waking at 3-4 AM (elevated cortisol from overtraining)
- Not feeling rested despite 8 hours

## Deload Weeks

Every 3-4 weeks, reduce training volume by 30-40% while keeping some intensity.

**Purpose of deload:**
- Allows connective tissue (tendons, ligaments) to catch up with cardiovascular adaptation
- Replenishes glycogen stores and hormonal reserves
- Reduces psychological fatigue and restores motivation

**How to deload:**
- Drop total weekly km by 30-40%
- Keep 1 quality session (tempo or short intervals) but at reduced volume
- Remove long run or shorten it significantly
- Add an extra rest day or replace runs with swimming/cycling

**Signs you need an unplanned deload:**
- Legs feel heavy for 3+ consecutive days
- Motivation to train has dropped significantly for 5+ days
- Resting HR elevated 5-10 bpm above baseline consistently

## Active Recovery Methods

| Method | Best For | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Easy jogging (Z1) | Flushing metabolites, maintaining movement | 20-30 min next day |
| Swimming / pool running | Full body recovery without impact | 30-45 min |
| Cycling (easy) | Lower limb recovery without impact | 30-60 min |
| Foam rolling | Reducing tissue tightness, IT band, quads | 10-15 min |
| Cold water immersion | Reducing acute inflammation post-race | 10-15 min at 10-15°C |
| Compression garments | Reducing DOMS, improving circulation | Wear for 2-4 hours post-run |

## Hard/Easy Day Structure

Never schedule two hard training days back-to-back. A hard day is any of:
- Long run (over 90 min)
- Tempo run or lactate threshold session
- Interval/VO2max session
- Race

Minimum one easy or rest day between any two hard sessions. The easy day is not optional — it is part of the training plan.

**Sample week structure:**
- Monday: Rest or easy 30 min
- Tuesday: Intervals (hard)
- Wednesday: Easy 45-60 min
- Thursday: Tempo run (hard)
- Friday: Easy 30-40 min or rest
- Saturday: Long run (hard)
- Sunday: Easy 40-50 min or rest

## Recovery Week Planning in Training Blocks

| Training Block | Hard Weeks | Recovery Week |
|---|---|---|
| 3:1 pattern | 3 hard weeks | 1 recovery week (volume -30-40%) |
| 4:1 pattern (advanced) | 4 hard weeks | 1 recovery week |
| Pre-race (taper) | n/a | 2-3 week progressive taper |

Monitoring your resting heart rate daily and tracking subjective energy levels (1-10 scale) are the simplest tools for knowing whether to push or back off.
