# Running Injury Prevention

## Common Running Injuries

### Runner's Knee (Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome)
- **Location**: Pain around or behind the kneecap
- **Cause**: Weak hip abductors/glutes causing the knee to cave inward (valgus
  collapse); overstriding; sudden volume increase
- **Prevention**: Hip strengthening exercises (clamshells, side-lying leg raises,
  single-leg squats); shorten stride to land with foot closer to body's center of
  mass; follow the 10% mileage rule
- **When to rest**: Rest if pain exceeds 3/10 during a run, or if pain persists
  after 48 hours of rest. Do not run through sharp or worsening knee pain.

### Achilles Tendinopathy
- **Location**: Pain or stiffness at the back of the heel, along the Achilles tendon
- **Cause**: Sudden increase in speedwork or hill running; inadequate calf strength;
  wearing shoes with zero drop without gradual adaptation; training on hard surfaces
  without recovery
- **Prevention**: Eccentric calf raises (heel drops off a step, 3×15 reps daily);
  avoid sudden shifts to speedwork; warm up before hard sessions
- **When to rest**: Rest if the tendon feels "stiff" every morning for more than
  3 consecutive days, or if there is localized swelling. Achilles injuries worsen
  quickly if pushed through.

### Iliotibial Band Syndrome (IT Band Syndrome)
- **Location**: Sharp pain on the outside of the knee, typically appears after
  30–45 minutes of running
- **Cause**: Hip abductor weakness; excessive downhill running; sudden mileage
  increase; worn-out shoes
- **Prevention**: Hip strengthening (glute bridges, lateral band walks); avoid
  excessive camber running; foam roll the TFL (not the IT band itself); reduce
  downhill running in the first 8 weeks of a training cycle
- **When to rest**: Rest if pain starts before the 30-minute mark, or if you are
  compensating your gait to avoid pain.

## General Injury Prevention Principles

1. **Build mileage before intensity** — spend 4–6 weeks building base before adding
   speedwork or tempo runs to a training block.
2. **Strength training 2×/week** — hip, glute, and single-leg stability work
   dramatically reduces injury risk for runners.
3. **Rotate shoes** — alternating between two pairs extends shoe life and varies
   the stress pattern on your feet and legs.
4. **Listen to body signals** — distinguish between normal training fatigue (symmetrical
   heaviness that goes away after 20 minutes of easy running) and injury warning signs
   (sharp, localized, asymmetrical, or worsening pain).

## When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention if:
- Pain is sharp, localized to one spot, and does not improve after 5–7 days of rest
- You notice visible swelling, bruising, or deformity
- You are limping during normal daily walking
- Pain wakes you from sleep
